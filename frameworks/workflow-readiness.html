Not Every Workflow Is Ready for AI

Readiness is a property of the workflow, not the model. The model will keep improving. Your workflow won't, until someone fixes it.

When an AI initiative stalls, teams blame the model: not accurate enough, hallucinated, didn't understand our business. Then a better model arrives and the initiative stalls again. The workflow it was dropped into was never ready to receive it.

The question teams ask

"Is the AI good enough?"

Leads to benchmark debates and waiting for the next release. Nothing about your operation changes.

The question that matters

"Is the workflow ready?"

Leads to six testable properties of the work itself. Each one is fixable without waiting for anyone's roadmap.

The Six Readiness Tests

A workflow is AI-ready when it passes all six. Each test takes minutes to run and points directly at what to fix.

TEST 1Defined

Can anyone describe this workflow end-to-end?

  • Inputs, steps, decisions, and outputs are nameable
  • Two people describe it roughly the same way
  • Exceptions are known, not discovered

Fails when: the process lives in one person's head, or every case is "it depends."

TEST 2Digitized

Do the inputs and outputs exist in systems?

  • The work arrives as data, documents, or tickets
  • Source systems are accessible
  • History exists to learn from

Fails when: the real workflow actually runs over calls and tribal knowledge.

TEST 3Verifiable

Can a human check the output faster than producing it?

  • Quality is judgeable at a glance or with a quick check
  • Review takes minutes; production takes hours
  • "Good" has criteria, not vibes

Fails when: verifying the AI's answer takes as long as doing the work.

TEST 4Recoverable

When the AI is wrong, can you catch and undo it?

  • Errors surface before they reach a customer or a regulator
  • A wrong output is an edit, not an incident
  • A human checkpoint exists where stakes are high

Fails when: a single bad output is irreversible or compliance-critical with no review step.

TEST 5Owned

Does one accountable person have authority to change this workflow?

  • A single person can alter the process without convening a committee
  • That owner wants the change
  • The people doing the work report into a decision path

Fails when: the workflow crosses three teams and belongs to none of them.

TEST 6Repeatable

Does this work happen often enough to matter?

  • Volume or frequency justifies the build and the change cost
  • Cases share structure — not 500 one-offs
  • Demand will exist next year

Fails when: it's a quarterly task someone does in an afternoon.

In practice, most workflows fail the same two tests: Verifiable and Owned. Teams obsess over data quality. What actually kills AI initiatives after launch is output nobody can check and a process nobody owns.

The Readiness Spectrum

Score the six tests and every workflow lands in one of three bands — each with a different correct move.

Not Ready

Fails Defined, Digitized, or Owned

The workflow itself is broken or invisible. Bolt AI onto it and you automate the chaos: faster wrong answers, at scale.

Move: fix the process first. This is a process project, not an AI project.

Ready With Work

Passes structure, fails Verifiable or Recoverable

The workflow is sound but unsafe to augment. Errors would slip through, or couldn't be undone.

Move: design the review checkpoint and rollback path, then proceed. This usually takes weeks.

Ready

Passes all six

Defined, digitized, checkable, recoverable, owned, and frequent. AI lands on solid ground here.

Move: proceed, with the baseline written down first (see Framework 01).

Amber is not a rejection, it's a short to-do list. Most of what gets called an AI failure was an amber workflow treated as green.

How to Use This

STEP 1

Pick the workflow

One workflow at a time. A department is too big to test.

STEP 2

Run the six tests

With the people who do the work. Managers describe the process as designed, not as it runs.

STEP 3

Band it

Red, amber, or green. A "probably" on any test is a fail.

STEP 4

Fix what failed

Each failed test names its own remediation. Re-test, then build.
